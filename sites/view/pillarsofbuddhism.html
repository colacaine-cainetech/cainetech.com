The Three Pillars of Buddhism

The Foundation of Buddhist Practice

üôè Buddha (The Awakened One)

The first pillar represents taking refuge in the Buddha, the historical teacher Siddhartha Gautama who attained enlightenment. This pillar encompasses respect for the Buddha as a teacher and guide, recognizing his profound wisdom and the path he discovered to liberation from suffering.

Beyond the historical figure, the Buddha also represents the potential for awakening that exists within all beings. Taking refuge in the Buddha means acknowledging this possibility and committing to following the path toward enlightenment.

üìø Dharma (The Teaching)

The second pillar is the Dharma, the teachings and doctrines of Buddhism. This includes the Four Noble Truths, the Eightfold Path, and all the wisdom transmitted by the Buddha regarding the nature of reality, suffering, and the path to liberation.

The Dharma is considered the medicine that cures the suffering of existence. It provides practical guidance for ethical living, mental cultivation, and the development of wisdom. Practitioners study and contemplate these teachings to understand the true nature of reality.

üßò Sangha (The Community)

The third pillar is the Sangha, traditionally referring to the monastic community of monks and nuns, but more broadly encompassing all practitioners who follow the Buddhist path. The Sangha provides spiritual friendship, support, and guidance for those on the journey.

The community serves as a living embodiment of Buddhist principles and offers practitioners the opportunity to learn from one another, practice together, and maintain their commitment to the path. In many traditions, the Sangha is considered essential for sustaining practice and realizing the teachings.

These Three Jewels (Buddha, Dharma, Sangha) form the foundation of Buddhist practice across all traditions.
